Most traders look at charts and guess where the price could go. This shows where the options markets actually pricing the spx to be in the coming weeks. Every SPX option is a bet on a future price. And when you combine thousands of those bets, you get a probability map where traders thinks price is more likely to end up. To build this, you take option prices across all price levels and create a smooth curve. Then you take the derivative which tells you how fast the option price changes when you move the strike slightly. When the option price drops very quickly around certain strikes, it means traders are cutting off a big chunk of the expected payoff, which only happens where the market thinks price is more likely to be. Doing this again, let us pull out the full probability distribution from the options. This give us the probability curve for a single expiration showing where the spx is more likely to finish in just a few days. Repeat this for many expiration dates and you get a forward probability surface that shows how the expected rate changes over time. From there, we can calculate what is the probability that SPX finishes above any level. You can literally see which price levels the market thinks SPX is likely to stay above as time passes. When you overlay these into price, you're trading probabilities instead of drawing random lines.
